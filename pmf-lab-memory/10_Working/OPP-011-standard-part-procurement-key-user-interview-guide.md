# OPP-011 Standard-Part Procurement Key User Interview Guide

## Interview Purpose

Clarify whether the current cylinder-selection agent is creating real procurement value, not only engineering convenience.

The interview should answer:

1. What the real standard-part procurement workflow looks like.
2. Who owns the decision on preferred models, supplier choice, and price comparison.
3. What procurement actually optimizes for in practice.
4. Whether agent-recommended models would be trusted and adopted by procurement stakeholders.

## Recommended Interviewee

Choose the closest real owner of one or more of these decisions:

- preferred-part policy
- supplier pool maintenance
- quotation or price comparison
- final purchase approval for standard parts

If no single owner exists, interview the most central coordinator first.

## Recommended Duration

- 45 to 60 minutes

## Suggested Opening

Explain that the goal is not to evaluate the interviewee personally.
The goal is to understand the current procurement workflow for standard parts, especially cylinders, and test whether an agent-assisted selection process could create real value.

## Core Question Set

### A. Workflow Reality

1. Please walk through the current standard-part procurement workflow for cylinders from engineering demand to purchase order.
2. At which step does procurement first get involved?
3. Which systems, files, or people are usually needed before a cylinder order can be placed?
4. Which parts of the workflow are stable and standardized, and which parts still depend on individual experience?

### B. Decision Ownership

5. Who decides the model selection in practice: engineering, procurement, or a shared approval chain?
6. Who owns the preferred-part list or preferred-supplier logic today?
7. Under what conditions can procurement challenge or override an engineering-selected model?
8. Who is judged on cost, lead time, supplier availability, and standardization?

### C. Supplier And Pricing Logic

9. When comparing cylinder options, what matters most in practice: technical fit, price, lead time, supplier relationship, stock availability, or standardization?
10. How is supplier optionality judged today?
11. How often do you encounter cases where the engineering-selected model is usable but not the best purchasing choice?
12. How do you currently compare price across equivalent or near-equivalent models?

### D. Pain And Failure Modes

13. What are the most common problems in current cylinder purchasing decisions?
14. Where do extra cost, delay, dead inventory, or non-standard selections usually come from?
15. Which errors are expensive enough that you would want system support before placing an order?

### E. Reaction To Agent Support

16. If an agent recommended a technically usable cylinder with lower price or more supplier options, what would make you trust or reject it?
17. What evidence would procurement need before adopting such recommendations in a real workflow?
18. Would procurement prefer the agent to recommend one best model, several ranked options, or one preferred model plus substitutes?
19. If the agent conflicts with engineering preference, how should that conflict be handled?

### F. Closing

20. If we run a small pilot, what would be the fairest way to judge whether the agent is helping procurement?
21. Who else should we talk to next to understand this workflow correctly?

## Evidence To Capture

- actual workflow steps
- decision owner by step
- approval chain
- procurement KPI priorities
- price comparison method
- supplier-option logic
- exception handling logic
- trust requirements for agent recommendations
- suggested next interview targets

## Signals To Listen For

### Positive signals

- procurement often receives engineering selections that are technically usable but purchasing-suboptimal
- there is recurring manual comparison of price, lead time, or supplier availability
- preferred-part policy exists but is inconsistently followed
- the interviewee asks for ranked alternatives or clearer tradeoff explanations

### Risk signals

- procurement has no practical influence on model choice
- price is not the real bottleneck and standardization already works well
- cylinder purchasing is too fragmented or too low volume to matter
- data needed for comparison is too messy or politically sensitive

## Close-Out Questions

- Can we access anonymized recent cylinder purchase cases for analysis?
- Can we identify the top 20 most frequently purchased cylinder models?
- Can we review the preferred-part list or supplier mapping if one exists?
- Who should approve a narrow pilot if the first evidence is positive?
